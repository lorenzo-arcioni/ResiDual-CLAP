\subsection{Spatial Aggregation and Dataset Representations}
\label{sec:aggregation}
% -------------------------------------------------------------

Each raw head output $\mathbf{H}_{\ell,b,h}$ is a three-dimensional tensor
indexed by window, token position, and feature dimension. To obtain a single
fixed-size vector per audio sample, we mean-pool over all windows and token
positions:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{r}_{\ell,b,h}
    = \frac{1}{N_w^\ell\, M}
      \sum_{i=1}^{N_w^\ell}\sum_{j=1}^{M}
      \mathbf{H}_{\ell,b,h}[i,j,:]
    \;\in\; \mathbb{R}^{d_h}.
    \label{eq:spatial_pooling}
\end{equation}
This yields one vector $\mathbf{r}_{\ell,b,h}$ per audio sample,
per head $h$, at every block $(\ell,b)$ of the network. Stacking these
vectors across the $n$ samples in the dataset gives the matrix
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{R}_{\ell,b,h} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_h},
\end{equation}
which is the primary object of our analysis. The total number of such
matrices across the model is $H_{\mathrm{tot}} = 184$ (one per head per
block; see Table~\ref{tab:architecture}).

Since $d_h = 24$ is constant across all stages, the ambient dimension of
$\mathbf{R}_{\ell,b,h}$ is uniform throughout the network, enabling direct
cross-stage comparisons of all dimensionality metrics without correction for
a varying ceiling.

\subsection{Intrinsic Dimensionality Analysis}
\label{sec:dimensionality_method}
% -------------------------------------------------------------

To characterize the effective complexity of the projected head representations
$\{\mathbf{r}_{\ell,b,h}^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^{n} \subset \mathbb{R}^{1024}$, we employ a battery of
linear and nonlinear dimensionality estimators.

\subsubsection{Linear Estimators}

\paragraph{PCA-based dimensionality.}
For each head, let $\mathbf{R}_{\ell,b,h} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_h}$ stack all aggregated
representations. We compute the covariance matrix $\mathbf{C}_{\ell,b,h} =
\tfrac{1}{n-1}\mathbf{R}^\top\mathbf{R}$ and obtain ordered eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2
\geq \cdots$. Linear intrinsic dimensionality is:
\begin{equation}
    d_{\mathrm{PCA}}(\alpha)
    = \arg\min_{k}\left\{
        \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k}\lambda_i}{\sum_{i}\lambda_i} \geq \alpha
      \right\},
    \label{eq:pca_dim}
\end{equation}
evaluated at $\alpha \in \{0.90, 0.95, 0.99\}$. We also report the \emph{Explained Variance Ratio}
of the first principal component, $\mathrm{EVR}_1 = \lambda_1 / \sum_i \lambda_i$.

\paragraph{Participation Ratio.}
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{PR}
    = \frac{\left(\sum_{i}\lambda_i\right)^2}{\sum_{i}\lambda_i^2}.
    \label{eq:pr}
\end{equation}
High PR signals uniform variance distribution; low PR signals dominance of few directions.

\paragraph{Effective Rank.}
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{EffRank} = \exp\!\left(-\sum_{i} p_i \log p_i\right),
    \quad p_i = \frac{\lambda_i}{\sum_j \lambda_j},
    \label{eq:effrank}
\end{equation}
which can be interpreted as the exponential of the spectral entropy. Unlike PCA thresholds, it
provides a smooth, threshold-free notion of dimensionality, capturing how uniformly variance is
distributed across components.

\subsubsection{Nonlinear Estimators}

\paragraph{TwoNN.}
\begin{equation}
    d_{\mathrm{TwoNN}}
    = \left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\log\frac{r_2^{(i)}}{r_1^{(i)}}\right)^{-1},
    \label{eq:twonn}
\end{equation}
where $r_1^{(i)}, r_2^{(i)}$ are Euclidean distances to the first and second nearest neighbours of
$\mathbf{r}_{\ell,b,h}^{(i)}$~\cite{facco2017twonearest}.

\paragraph{MLE.}
\begin{equation}
    \hat{d}_{\mathrm{MLE}}(\mathbf{r})
    = \left(\frac{1}{k-1}\sum_{j=1}^{k-1}\log\frac{r_k(\mathbf{r})}{r_j(\mathbf{r})}\right)^{-1},
    \label{eq:mle}
\end{equation}
averaged over all samples~\cite{levina2005mle}, with $k=20$. This estimator is based on a
local maximum likelihood principle, assuming uniform sampling in small neighbourhoods, and is
sensitive to fine-scale manifold structure beyond global linear approximations.

\paragraph{Linear-Nonlinear (L/N) Ratio}
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{Ratio}_B
    = \frac{\bar{d}_{\mathrm{PCA}_{99}}}{\bar{d}_{\mathrm{TwoNN}}}.
    \label{eq:ln_ratio}
\end{equation}
Values near 1 indicate near-linear manifolds; higher values signal nonlinear curvature beyond what
PCA captures, and serve as a diagnostic for selecting layer targets for spectral reweighting.

\subsubsection{Global Trends and Stage-wise Progression}
% -------------------------------------------------------------

Osservando la Figura~\ref{fig:blockwise_metrics}, emerge chiaramente come la complessità 
delle rappresentazioni aumenti progressivamente risalendo la gerarchia del modello. 

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Layer 0 (Expansion Phase):} Nei primi blocchi, la 
    dimensionalità intrinseca è minima ($L \approx 2.75$ nel blocco 0). 
    L'altissimo valore di $\mathrm{EVR1}$ ($0.92$) indica che quasi tutta la 
    varianza è concentrata in una singola direzione. Questo suggerisce che 
    i primi stadi agiscono come estrattori di feature a bassa complessità, 
    focalizzandosi su segnali dominanti del dominio tempo-frequenza.
    \item \textbf{Layer 1 \& 2 (Complexity Growth):} Si osserva una crescita 
    rapida di $L$ e $N$. Il rapporto $L/N$ raggiunge il suo picco nel Layer 2 ($2.65$), 
    suggerendo che in questa fase la curvatura del manifold è massima. 
    Le rappresentazioni non solo occupano più dimensioni, ma lo fanno in 
    modo significativamente non lineare rispetto alle basi PCA.
    \item \textbf{Layer 3 (Saturation and Refinement):} Negli ultimi blocchi (10-11), 
    la dimensionalità tende a stabilizzarsi o a subire una leggera contrazione 
    (specialmente in $N$). La $\mathrm{EVR1}$ crolla ai minimi storici ($0.15$), 
    indicando una distribuzione della varianza estremamente uniforme tra le componenti 
    disponibili ($d_h=24$).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Head Specialization Analysis}
\label{sec:specialization_method}
% -------------------------------------------------------------

Per interpretare il contenuto semantico codificato dalle singole teste, introduciamo un framework di \textit{cross-modal grounding} basato su un modello CLAP pre-addestrato. L'obiettivo è mappare l'asse di varianza principale di ogni testa in uno spazio di concetti testuali descrittivi.

\subsubsection{Semantic Mapping via Principal Component Projection}

Sia $\mathbf{R}_{\ell,b,h} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d_h}$ la matrice delle rappresentazioni aggregate per una data testa. Per identificare la caratteristica acustica primaria catturata dalla testa, calcoliamo la prima componente principale $\mathbf{v}_{1} \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h}$. 
Per ogni campione $i$, lo score sulla prima componente è definito come:
\begin{equation}
    s_{i} = \mathbf{r}_{\ell,b,h}^{(i)} \cdot \mathbf{v}_{1}.
\end{equation}

Definiamo quindi la \textbf{Direzione Semantica} $\mathbf{\sigma}_{\ell,b,h}$ nello spazio latente CLAP come la combinazione lineare degli embedding audio CLAP $\{\mathbf{a}_i\}_{i=1}^n \subset \mathbb{R}^{D_{\mathrm{CLAP}}}$, pesata dagli score normalizzati:
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{\sigma}_{\ell,b,h} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i \mathbf{a}_i, \quad \text{con } w_i = \frac{s_i - \bar{s}}{\|\mathbf{s} - \bar{s}\|_2},
\end{equation}
dove $\bar{s}$ è la media degli score. Questo vettore $\mathbf{\sigma}$ rappresenta l'asse di massima variazione della testa proiettato in uno spazio allineato al linguaggio naturale.

\subsubsection{Sparse Coding via Orthogonal Matching Pursuit (OMP)}

Per tradurre $\mathbf{\sigma}_{\ell,b,h}$ in termini comprensibili, utilizziamo un dizionario testuale $\mathcal{D}$ composto da $M$ descrizioni audio (es. "dog barking", "high pitched tone"). Siano $\{\mathbf{t}_j\}_{j=1}^M$ i corrispondenti embedding testuali CLAP.
Poiché una testa potrebbe rispondere a una combinazione di concetti, decomponiamo $\mathbf{\sigma}$ mediante un algoritmo \textit{Orthogonal Matching Pursuit} (OMP). L'OMP seleziona iterativamente i $k$ atomi dal dizionario che meglio spiegano la direzione semantica, risolvendo:
\begin{equation}
    \min_{\mathbf{x}} \|\mathbf{\sigma}_{\ell,b,h} - \mathbf{T}\mathbf{x}\|_2 \quad \text{s.t. } \|\mathbf{x}\|_0 \leq k,
\end{equation}
dove $\mathbf{T}$ è la matrice degli embedding del dizionario. Nel nostro esperimento fissiamo $k=5$. Questo approccio "greedy" permette di distinguere tra teste altamente specializzate (che puntano a un singolo atomo) e teste che catturano concetti acustici più ampi.

\subsubsection{Metrics for Semantic Coherence}

Per quantificare la qualità della specializzazione, introduciamo lo \textbf{Z-score di Coerenza}. Data la sottorete di $k$ descrizioni selezionate $\mathcal{D}_k \subset \mathcal{D}$, definiamo la similarità intra-gruppo $S_{\mathrm{intra}}$ come la media dei prodotti scalari tra gli embedding selezionati. 
Lo Z-score è calcolato come:
\begin{equation}
    Z = \frac{S_{\mathrm{intra}} - \mu_{\mathcal{D}}}{\sigma_{\mathcal{D}}},
    \label{eq:coherence_z}
\end{equation}
dove $\mu_{\mathcal{D}}$ e $\sigma_{\mathcal{D}}$ sono rispettivamente la media e la deviazione standard delle similarità tra gli atomi selezionati e l'intero dizionario $\mathcal{D}$. Un valore di $Z$ elevato indica che la testa è focalizzata su un dominio semantico ben definito e consistente (es. solo suoni naturali o solo parametri timbrici), mentre valori bassi indicano una specializzazione spuria o eccessivamente eterogenea.

\subsection{Results: Semantic Landscapes and Head Specialization}
\label{sec:specialization_results}
% -------------------------------------------------------------

L'applicazione del framework di \textit{cross-modal grounding} rivela l'emergere 
di una specializzazione funzionale che segue la gerarchia strutturale del modello. 
L'analisi dello Z-score di coerenza semantica (Figura~\ref{fig:coherence_heatmap}) 
e l'identificazione delle teste più specializzate (Tabella~\ref{tab:top_specialized_heads}) 
permettono di capire come i concetti acustici astratti si cristallizzino in categorie semantiche.

% -------------------------------------------------------------
\subsubsection{Emergence of Semantic Coherence}
% -------------------------------------------------------------

L'andamento della coerenza semantica attraverso i layer (Figura~\ref{fig:coherence_heatmap}) 
rivela una transizione di fase legata alla struttura gerarchica del modello:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Layer 0 e 1 (Semantic Void):} Nei primi quattro blocchi (0-3), la 
    coerenza semantica è prevalentemente negativa o prossima allo zero. In questa fase, 
    le teste estraggono feature a bassa dimensionalità ($L < 14$) e altissima dominanza 
    spettrale ($\text{EVR}_1 \approx 0.92$). La bassa coerenza i
    ndica che queste teste rispondono a proprietà fisiche del segnale 
    comuni a molteplici sorgenti, 
    rendendo impossibile un mapping univoco su descrizioni testuali specifiche.
    \item \textbf{Layer 2 (Functional Diversification):} This stage marks
    the transition into a regime of increased non-linearity. In particular,
    a clear increase in head specialization emerges toward the end of the layer,
    in concomitance with the growth of the intrinsic non-linear dimensionality
    (as measured by TwoNN, Fig.~\ref{fig:blockwise_metrics}) and the concurrent
    decrease in the leading eigenvalue share (EVR$_1$), which drops from $0.24$
    at the beginning of Layer~2 to $0.16$ at its end (Fig.~\ref{fig:blockwise_metrics}).

    This joint behaviour suggests that the expansion of the underlying manifold
    complexity is closely linked to the emergence of more semantically selective
    attention heads. Accordingly, highly specialized heads begin to appear,
    such as \texttt{L2\_B4\_H1} ($Z=1.46$, absolute block 8), which capture
    fine-grained acoustic events associated with the \textit{human\_body} category.
    \item \textbf{Layer 3 (Semantic Convergence):} This layer initially continues the trend
    observed at the end of Layer 2, with a high density of more specialized heads in the
    early blocks of Layer 3. In this regime, heads remain well-aligned with CLAP semantic
    concepts, despite the fact that the leading eigenvalue share (EVR$_1$) reaches one of its
    lowest values ($0.15$), indicating that information is distributed across a larger number
    of latent components.

    However, this trend progressively reverses toward the final blocks (10--11), where a
    mild decrease in head specialization is observed. This reversal is consistent with a
    broader inversion in all structural metrics, including $L$, $N$, $L/N$, and EVR$_1$,
    which collectively shift from their previously increasing or decreasing trends
    (see Fig.~\ref{fig:blockwise_metrics}). 

    Overall, Layer~3 appears to exhibit an initial phase of strong semantic alignment,
    followed by a late-stage relaxation of specialization, suggesting a partial
    re-mixing of representations in the deepest blocks.
\end{itemize}

The results reported in Table~\ref{tab:top_specialized_heads} confirm the emergence of a
clear semantic organization of attention heads across layers. In particular, the highest
coherence scores are consistently associated with heads that capture well-defined acoustic
categories such as \textit{human\_body}, \textit{nature}, and \textit{urban sounds},
suggesting that specialization is not random but structurally aligned with meaningful
audio concepts.

A notable trend is the predominance of highly coherent heads in Layers~2 and~3,
supporting the observation that semantic specialization intensifies after the initial
low-level feature extraction stage. Moreover, heads with higher Z-scores tend to exhibit
lower PC1 variance, indicating that stronger semantic alignment correlates with more
concentrated spectral structure.

Finally, the presence of moderately coherent but still structured heads in later blocks
suggests that, while full specialization emerges primarily in intermediate layers, the
deepest representations undergo a partial re-mixing of features, consistent with the
non-monotonic behaviour observed in Fig.~\ref{fig:coherence_heatmap} and
Fig.~\ref{fig:blockwise_metrics}. Importantly, the observed inversion of the trend in the
deepest blocks is also consistent with the findings reported in \cite{residual_paper},
indicating a shared structural behaviour across architectures and suggesting that this
non-monotonic specialization pattern extends beyond the audio domain to image-domain
residual stream models as well.
