\section{Related Works}

\paragraph{Spectral Decomposition in Transformers.}
The ResiDual framework formalizes residual streams through 
eigenspace decomposition, showing that attention heads operate within constrained, 
low-dimensional subspaces capturing specific semantic roles. 
This aligns with Voita et al.~\cite{Voita2019}, who showed that only a subset 
of heads is critical for performance while others can be pruned with minimal impact.

\paragraph{Audio–Text Models and CLAP.}
CLAP has emerged as a leading architecture for joint audio-text 
embeddings, building on CLIP-like methods~\cite{radford2021clip} and 
attention-based audio models~\cite{gong2021ast}. 
Despite these advances, internal representation geometry within residual 
pathways remains underexplored: prior studies examined attention 
distributions~\cite{understandingselfattention, studyofattention, musicattention} 
or audio embeddings~\cite{audioembeddings}, but not the spectral properties 
of residual streams. To the best of my knowledge, this is the first systematic 
spectral analysis and reweighting strategy applied to CLAP.

\paragraph{Spectral Reweighting and Representation Geometry.}
Pretrained models are known to produce anisotropic representations, 
degrading cosine-similarity-based comparisons~\cite{contextual}. 
Post-hoc corrections such as All-but-the-Top~\cite{allbutthetop} and 
whitening~\cite{su2021whitening} address this at the output embedding level. 
ResiDual showed that reweighting principal components 
of attention head outputs — rather than output embeddings — yields more targeted 
spectral alignment. I extend this paradigm to the audio domain, applying it to the HTS-AT within CLAP.
